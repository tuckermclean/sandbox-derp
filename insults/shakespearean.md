# Shakespearean Insults

> A curated arsenal of abuse, sourced from the canon. Every line below was deployed in
> production by William Shakespeare himself — battle-tested across four centuries with
> zero reported regressions.

The plays are, among other things, the most quotable collection of insults in the English
language. What follows is a genuine list, attributed to its source where the attribution
is natural, plus the famous build-your-own kit at the end.

## Single-Strike Insults

Short, sharp, and ready to deploy.

- "Thou art a boil, a plague sore." — *King Lear*
- "Away, you starvelling, you elf-skin, you dried neat's-tongue, you bull's-pizzle, you stock-fish!" — *Henry IV, Part 1*
- "Thou art as fat as butter." — *Henry IV, Part 1*
- "You are not worth another word, else I'd call you knave." — *All's Well That Ends Well*
- "I do desire we may be better strangers." — *As You Like It*
- "Thou clay-brained guts, thou knotty-pated fool, thou whoreson obscene greasy tallow-catch!" — *Henry IV, Part 1*
- "More of your conversation would infect my brain." — *Coriolanus*
- "Thou art a flesh-monger, a fool, and a coward." — *Measure for Measure*
- "Thou cream-faced loon!" — *Macbeth*
- "Would thou wert clean enough to spit upon!" — *Timon of Athens*
- "Thine face is not worth sunburning." — *Henry V*
- "Thou art unfit for any place but hell." — *Richard III*
- "Out of my sight! Thou dost infect mine eyes." — *Richard III*
- "There's no more faith in thee than in a stewed prune." — *Henry IV, Part 1*

## Insults of Some Length

For when a single line will not suffice.

- "Were I like thee, I'd throw away myself." — *Timon of Athens*
- "I am sick when I do look on thee." — *A Midsummer Night's Dream*
- "Thou art a very ragged wart." — *Henry IV, Part 2*
- "His wit's as thick as Tewkesbury mustard." — *Henry IV, Part 2*
- "You scullion! You rampallian! You fustilarian! I'll tickle your catastrophe!" — *Henry IV, Part 2*
- "Thou whoreson zed, thou unnecessary letter!" — *King Lear*
- "Methink'st thou art a general offence and every man should beat thee." — *All's Well That Ends Well*
- "That trunk of humours, that bolting-hutch of beastliness, that swollen parcel of dropsies, that huge bombard of sack, that stuffed cloak-bag of guts." — *Henry IV, Part 1*
- "Thou art like a foul wind whose breath blows down the limit of his sin." — *Antony and Cleopatra* (paraphrase of Enobarbus's barbs)
- "The tartness of his face sours ripe grapes." — *Coriolanus*

## A Note on Authenticity

Lines marked with a play title are genuine Shakespeare. The insult kit is a modern
parlor game built from Shakespearean vocabulary rather than a single quoted line — its
words are period-appropriate, but the combinations are yours to assemble. Use
responsibly. Or, in keeping with house style, do not use at all, at scale.
